# Sustainable Supply Chain Strategy
## Pacari – Premium Organic Chocolate Value Chain Analysis

---

## Project Overview

This project analyzes the **global cocoa and chocolate value chain** using the case of **Pacari**, a premium organic chocolate manufacturer based in Ecuador.

The objective is to understand how a company operating in a traditionally **commodity-exporting country** can capture more value by moving upstream and downstream in the supply chain while maintaining strong social and environmental sustainability practices.

The project focuses on three strategic questions:

1. Which actors capture the largest share of value in the global chocolate value chain?
2. What business model generates greater economic, social, and environmental value in Ecuador?
3. Should Pacari vertically integrate by opening its own retail stores?

The case illustrates how supply chain strategy can transform a traditional commodity export industry into a **high value-added product ecosystem**.

---

## Global Cocoa Value Chain

The chocolate industry operates through a multi-stage international supply chain involving actors across several continents.

### Main Supply Chain Stages

Farmers → Intermediaries → Industrial Processors → Chocolate Manufacturers → Retailers

### Key Players

| Stage | Role |
|------|------|
| Farmers | Grow cocoa beans |
| Intermediaries | Aggregate and trade raw cocoa |
| Processors | Produce cocoa liquor, butter, and powder |
| Manufacturers | Produce chocolate products |
| Retailers | Sell final products to consumers |

This structure creates significant **value asymmetry**, where upstream actors capture only a small fraction of final product value.

---

## Value Distribution Across the Supply Chain

Data from the Cocoa Barometer illustrates how value increases as cocoa moves downstream.

| Supply Chain Stage | Cost | Selling Value |
|--------------------|------|---------------|
| Farmers | $664 | $1,874 |
| Trading / Export | $1,874 | $3,038 |
| Industrial Processing | $3,038 | $4,434 |
| Chocolate Manufacturing | $4,434 | $10,858 |
| Retail | $10,858 | $18,917 |

This demonstrates that the **largest value capture occurs at manufacturing and retail stages**, not at agricultural production.

## Cocoa Industry Structure

The global chocolate industry is highly concentrated.

### Cocoa Processing

Five companies control more than **50% of the cocoa processing market**:

- Cargill
- ADM
- Barry Callebaut
- Petra Foods
- Bloomer

### Chocolate Manufacturing

The chocolate manufacturing market is also dominated by large multinational corporations:

- Kraft
- Mars
- Nestlé
- Hershey
- Ferrero

These firms capture a large share of the value through **branding, marketing, and distribution capabilities**.

## Ecuador in the Global Cocoa Economy

Ecuador plays a unique role in the global cocoa industry.

Key characteristics include:

- One of the world’s major cocoa producers
- Global leader in **fine aroma cocoa**
- Holds approximately **63% of the fine aroma cocoa market**

However, historically Ecuador has exported mostly **raw cocoa beans rather than finished chocolate products**.

### Export Structure (2015)

| Product Category | Export Value |
|------------------|-------------|
| Cocoa Beans | $785M |
| Intermediate Products | $21M |
| Finished Chocolate | $56M |

This highlights how most value creation historically occurred **outside Ecuador**.

## Pacari’s Business Model

Pacari was founded to transform Ecuador’s role in the chocolate value chain by producing **premium organic chocolate** using local cocoa.

### Core Principles

- Direct sourcing from farmers
- Organic certified cocoa production
- Full control of manufacturing processes
- Premium positioning in international markets

Pacari sources cocoa from small farms of **2–5 hectares**, supporting approximately **3,500 farming families**.

---

## Sustainable Sourcing Strategy

Pacari implemented a supply chain model based on **direct relationships with farmers**.

Key practices include:

- Paying premium prices for organic cocoa
- Providing microloans and technical training
- Supporting agricultural education programs
- Promoting environmentally sustainable farming methods

Example price comparison:

- Market price for cocoa: **$1.90/kg**
- Price paid by Pacari for certified organic cocoa: **$4.00/kg**

This pricing strategy improves farmer income while ensuring high product quality and traceability.

---

## Vertical Integration Strategy

Pacari maintains control over key stages of production.

### Integrated Activities

- Cocoa bean sourcing
- Roasting and milling
- Chocolate manufacturing
- International distribution

This vertical integration allows the company to:

- maintain strict quality control
- ensure product traceability
- capture higher value from finished products

Pacari exports its chocolate to **more than 27 countries**, with Europe as its largest export market.

---

## Competitive Landscape

Two major competitors illustrate alternative business models in Ecuador.

### República del Cacao

- Works with ~3,000 farmers
- Operates boutique retail stores
- Focuses on origin-based chocolate branding

### Caoni

- Focuses primarily on domestic market
- Outsources manufacturing
- Emphasizes flavored premium chocolate products

These competitors demonstrate different strategies for capturing value within Ecuador’s chocolate industry.

---

## Strategic Decision: Retail Integration

A key strategic question is whether Pacari should open **wholly-owned retail stores**.

### Potential Benefits

- Greater control over brand positioning
- Higher margins through direct-to-consumer sales
- Stronger storytelling around sustainability and origin

### Potential Risks

- High fixed retail operating costs
- Increased management complexity
- Potential distraction from core manufacturing capabilities

---

## Key Insights

Several insights emerge from the analysis.

- Most value in the chocolate supply chain is captured downstream.
- Vertical integration enables firms to capture more value from finished products.
- Sustainable sourcing strengthens brand differentiation in premium markets.
- Consumers increasingly value traceability and origin-labeled products.
